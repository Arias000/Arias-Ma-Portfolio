REGENERATION OF WETLAND

Nanhui Dongtan, Lingang New City, Shanghai, China

Service

Landscape Architecture

Sector

Academic

Client

—

Size

Approx. 47,000 acres

Overview

The project is located in the Lingang New City Reclamation Area in Shanghai, China, specifically the southern section of the Nanhui Dongtan Wetland at the Yangtze River Estuary.

The site has a history of high-intensity reclamation, notably the 2002 construction of a 10 km-long flood control seawall that enclosed 47,000 acres of land for urban development.

Challenge

Ecosystem Destruction and Fragmentation: Large-scale reclamation transformed natural coastal wetlands into arable land, construction sites, and aquaculture ponds, leading to a fragmented landscape and threatened ecological health.

Disconnected Ecosystem: The existing concrete dams block natural water flow, which simultaneously increases flood risks and allows tides to elevate soil salinity and alkalinity.

The “Artificial Desert” Issue: Under a political mandate to plant 10,000 m2 of trees annually, wetlands were artificially converted into protective forests. Because the environment was unsuitable, these areas became ecological “deserts,” destroying the original wetland system.

Severe Salinization: The coastal soil suffers from extreme salinity and abnormal pH levels, with a lack of effective management to dilute the salt.

Solution

The design proposes a three-stage strategy for exploration and implementation:

Connecting the Ecological System: Through physical and water flow simulations (modeling sand dunes and island formation), the design creates micro-topography to mitigate tidal power and reshape the coastline. This involves removing parts of the dam and excavating soil to create islands and diverse habitats.

Salt Layer Isolation and Fresh Water Collection: Using a “salt washing” technique, water pressure is applied to penetrate surface salt into deeper soil layers. Underground isolation layers are set up to prevent sea salt intrusion, and ponds are created to collect acidic rainwater to neutralize the alkaline soil.

Plant Succession Strategy: An 8-year phased planting plan was developed. It begins with salt-tolerant halophytes and progresses through salt-absorption and salt-secretion plants to eventually achieve a diverse, resilient vegetation community.

Result

Ecological Restoration: The project establishes an integrated ecosystem consisting of a wood garden, fresh water wetlands, and sea water wetlands.

Environmental Improvement: Phased implementation leads to a significant increase in vegetation coverage, a decrease in soil salinity, and an enhanced rate of biological habitats.

Landscape and Social Value: The design incorporates wood paths, landscape benches, and viewing platforms. These features provide high-quality public spaces and natural beauty for the city while maintaining the restored ecological functions.
