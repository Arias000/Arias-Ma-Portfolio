Overview

The project, titled “Ambience Sensation: On Culture and Weather,” is a comprehensive research proposal exploring the relationship between climate change, human migration, and plant culture (specifically the peony).

The study spans from a global scale down to local contexts in Henan Province, China, and ultimately focuses on Nur-Sultan, Kazakhstan. The core concept involves migrating the culturally significant peony and its associated heritage from Henan to Kazakhstan as the environment in China deteriorates, aiming to facilitate the breeding and rebirth of a “sense of home” for migrants.

Challenge

The project addresses dual environmental challenges at both the origin and the destination:

Environmental Degradation at the Origin: The author’s hometown in Henan faces extreme weather events (such as severe flooding) and significant air pollution (PM10, PM2.5, etc.). Future climate predictions indicate an increase in drought intensity. These abiotic stresses directly threaten peonies by potential water loss, hindered nutrient uptake, inhibited cell division, and reduced photosynthesis.

Urban and Climatic Issues at the Destination: Nur-Sultan is characterized by low walkability (due to large road scales and rigid urban grids), poor landscape quality, and extreme climatic conditions including severe winter cold and very high wind speeds. High solar radiation and low precipitation further create barriers to the comfort of both the newly introduced plants and the human migrants.

Solution

To address these challenges, the project proposes a cross-border migration and landscape design strategy:

Species and Human Migration: Leveraging Kazakhstan’s policies to attract talent, the project envisions high-intellectuals from Henan migrating to Nur-Sultan with peony seeds preserved in ceramic or glass jars.

Site Selection and Functional Zoning: The chosen site is a botanical garden located within Nur-Sultan’s “Green Corridor,” utilizing its scientific platform to support peony introduction. The site is divided into functional zones: peony areas, event gathering plazas, winter wind barrier zones, and summer cooling areas.

Culturally-Based Biomimetic Design: Geometric elements are extracted from peony petals and traditional rug patterns. Through simulation studies on wind speed reduction, these elements are transformed into terrain obstacles designed to effectively block harsh winter winds.

Microclimate Regulation: The design integrates vegetation, topography, and a bio-water garden to lower summer temperatures and increase humidity through the principle of evaporative cooling.

Result

Through these landscape design interventions, the project achieves multiple objectives:

Improved Microclimate: It successfully creates comfortable outdoor spaces by effectively reducing wind speeds and regulating temperature and humidity. For example, the terrain design blocks extreme winter winds, while water features provide cooling in the summer.

Diverse Public Spaces: The project establishes rich landscape features such as “Lake Heart Island,” “Relax Plazas,” and “Forest Walking Paths,” providing comfortable resting and social venues for both migrants and local residents.

Cultural Preservation and Rebirth: By ensuring the survival and potential development of new peony varieties in a new environment, the project helps migrants reshape their sense of belonging and cultural identity in a foreign land.
